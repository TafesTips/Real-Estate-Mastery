6.1 Tenant Screening and Selection

Finding and keeping quality tenants is one of the most important aspects of successful real estate investing. A thorough screening process helps minimize vacancies, reduce turnover, and prevent costly evictions.

Creating a Tenant Screening Process

A systematic approach to tenant screening includes these key steps:

  1. Pre-Screening: Initial phone conversation to determine basic qualifications
  2. Property Showing: Observe how prospective tenants interact with the property
  3. Application: Collect detailed information and required documentation
  4. Verification: Check employment, income, and rental history
  5. Background Checks: Review credit, criminal, and eviction history
  6. Decision: Apply consistent criteria to accept or deny
  7. Documentation: Maintain records of all screening activities

Tenant Screening Criteria

Establish clear, consistent criteria that comply with fair housing laws:

  • Income Verification: Typically 2.5-3x monthly rent
  • Employment History: Stable employment (usually 6+ months)
  • Credit Score: Minimum threshold (often 600-650)
  • Rental History: No prior evictions or serious complaints
  • Criminal Background: Policy on types of offenses and timeframes
  • References: Positive feedback from previous landlords

Important: Apply criteria consistently to all applicants to avoid fair housing violations.

Fair Housing Compliance

Federal fair housing laws prohibit discrimination based on protected classes. Understanding these laws is essential for all landlords:

Federal Protected Classes
  • Race
  • Color
  • National Origin
  • Religion
  • Sex (including gender identity and sexual orientation)
  • Familial Status
  • Disability
Best Practices for Compliance
  • Document screening criteria in writing
  • Apply criteria consistently to all applicants
  • Maintain records of all applications and decisions
  • Provide written explanation for denials
  • Train all staff on fair housing requirements
  • Stay updated on local fair housing laws (which may include additional protected classes)

Using Tenant Screening Services

Professional screening services can provide comprehensive reports and help ensure compliance:

Types of Screening Reports
  • Credit Reports: Payment history, debts, and credit score
  • Criminal Background Checks: National and local records
  • Eviction History: Previous eviction filings and judgments
  • Income Verification: Employment and income confirmation
  • Rental History: Verification from previous landlords
Popular Screening Services
  • TransUnion SmartMove
  • RentPrep
  • Avail
  • MyRental
  • RentSpree
  • TurboTenant

Note: Many services allow you to pass the screening fee to applicants, which is standard practice in most markets.

6.2 Lease Agreements and Rent Collection

A comprehensive lease agreement and efficient rent collection system are foundational to successful property management.

Essential Lease Components

A well-drafted lease should include these key elements:

  • Names of All Parties: All adult occupants as tenants
  • Property Description: Address and specific unit
  • Term: Start and end dates, renewal options
  • Rent: Amount, due date, payment methods, late fees
  • Security Deposit: Amount, conditions for return
  • Utilities: Responsibility for each utility
  • Occupancy Limits: Maximum number of residents
  • Pet Policy: Allowed pets, deposits, fees
  • Maintenance Responsibilities: Tenant vs. landlord
  • Entry Rights: Notice requirements for landlord access
  • Prohibited Activities: Illegal activities, noise restrictions
  • Termination Conditions: Early termination fees, notice requirements

Lease Addendums and Disclosures

Additional documents that may be required or recommended:

  • Lead-Based Paint Disclosure: Required for pre-1978 properties
  • Mold Disclosure: Information about mold and prevention
  • Bed Bug Addendum: Prevention and reporting procedures
  • Crime-Free Addendum: Prohibits criminal activity
  • Pet Addendum: Detailed pet policies and fees
  • Smoking Policy: Designated areas or prohibition
  • Move-In Inspection: Documents property condition
  • Renter's Insurance Requirement: Coverage expectations
  • Satellite Dish/Cable Installation: Installation guidelines
  • Pool/Spa Rules: Safety and usage guidelines
  • State-Specific Disclosures: Vary by location

Note: Always use state-specific lease forms or have an attorney review your lease to ensure compliance with local laws.

Rent Collection Systems

Establishing an efficient rent collection system reduces late payments and administrative burden:

Traditional Collection Methods
  • Personal Check: Convenient but risk of bounced checks
  • Money Order/Cashier's Check: Guaranteed funds but inconvenient
  • Cash: Immediate but risky and requires receipt
  • Drop Box: Convenient but less secure
  • Mail: Easy but potential for delays
Digital Collection Methods
  • ACH Transfers: Automatic bank transfers
  • Online Payment Portals: Dedicated rent collection platforms
  • Mobile Payment Apps: Venmo, Zelle, PayPal
  • Credit/Debit Card Payments: Convenient but fees
  • Property Management Software: All-in-one solutions
Popular Rent Collection Platforms
  • Buildium: Comprehensive property management software with rent collection
  • Avail: Online rent collection with automatic reminders
  • RentTrack: Reports payments to credit bureaus to help tenants build credit
  • PayRent: Dedicated rent collection with flexible payment options
  • Zillow Rental Manager: Integrated with listing and application tools

Handling Late Payments and Evictions

Even with careful screening, late payments and evictions may occasionally be necessary:

Late Payment Process
  1. Send reminder before due date
  2. Assess late fee per lease terms
  3. Send formal late notice
  4. Contact tenant personally
  5. Consider payment plan if appropriate
  6. Issue pay-or-quit notice if necessary
  7. Begin eviction process as last resort
Eviction Process Overview
  1. Issue proper written notice (varies by state)
  2. File eviction lawsuit if tenant doesn't comply
  3. Attend court hearing
  4. Obtain judgment for possession
  5. Schedule removal with sheriff/constable
  6. Conduct move-out inspection
  7. Pursue unpaid rent if necessary

Important: Eviction laws vary significantly by location. Always follow local procedures exactly and consider consulting an attorney.

6.3 Maintenance and Repairs

Effective maintenance strategies protect your investment, keep tenants satisfied, and prevent small issues from becoming expensive problems.

Preventative Maintenance

Regular preventative maintenance saves money in the long run:

Seasonal Maintenance Schedule
  • Spring:
    • Check/clean air conditioning systems
    • Inspect roof and gutters
    • Test smoke/CO detectors
    • Check for pest issues
  • Summer:
    • Inspect irrigation systems
    • Check for water leaks
    • Trim vegetation away from structure
    • Clean dryer vents
  • Fall:
    • Service heating systems
    • Clean gutters
    • Check weatherstripping
    • Inspect roof before winter
  • Winter:
    • Insulate exposed pipes
    • Check for ice dams
    • Test emergency systems
    • Inspect for water intrusion

Maintenance Request Systems

An efficient system for handling maintenance requests improves tenant satisfaction:

Key Components
  • Clear Reporting Process: Multiple ways for tenants to submit requests (online portal, email, phone)
  • Request Prioritization:
    • Emergency: Same day (fire, flood, gas leak, no heat in winter)
    • Urgent: 24-48 hours (no hot water, refrigerator failure)
    • Routine: 3-7 days (minor repairs, cosmetic issues)
  • Documentation: Track all requests and resolutions
  • Follow-up: Confirm tenant satisfaction after completion
  • Analysis: Review patterns to identify recurring issues
Maintenance Request Tools
  • Property management software with maintenance modules
  • Dedicated maintenance apps (Property Meld, Latchel)
  • Simple online forms (Google Forms, JotForm)
  • Email templates for consistent communication

Building a Vendor Network

Developing relationships with reliable service providers is essential for efficient property management:

Essential Vendors
  • General Handyman: For minor repairs and maintenance
  • Plumber: For water and sewer issues
  • Electrician: For electrical repairs and upgrades
  • HVAC Technician: For heating and cooling systems
  • Roofer: For roof repairs and replacement
  • Locksmith: For lock changes and security issues
  • Pest Control: For insect and rodent issues
  • Landscaper: For exterior maintenance
  • Cleaning Service: For turnover cleaning
  • Carpet Cleaner: For deep cleaning between tenants
  • Appliance Repair: For kitchen and laundry equipment
Vendor Management Best Practices
  • Vetting Process:
    • Verify licenses and insurance
    • Check references and reviews
    • Start with small jobs to test reliability
    • Compare pricing with multiple quotes
  • Relationship Building:
    • Pay promptly
    • Communicate clearly
    • Provide consistent work
    • Express appreciation
  • Documentation:
    • Maintain vendor contact information
    • Keep records of all work performed
    • Track warranties and guarantees
    • Document pricing agreements

Turnover Process

Efficient turnover procedures minimize vacancy periods and prepare units properly for new tenants:

  1. Move-Out Inspection:
    • Schedule with departing tenant
    • Compare to move-in condition report
    • Document damage beyond normal wear and tear
    • Take detailed photos
  2. Security Deposit Settlement:
    • Calculate legitimate deductions
    • Provide itemized statement
    • Return remaining deposit within legal timeframe
  3. Turnover Cleaning and Repairs:
    • Deep clean all surfaces
    • Replace worn or damaged items
    • Fresh paint if needed
    • Professional carpet cleaning
    • Address all maintenance issues
  4. Property Improvements:
    • Evaluate potential upgrades
    • Consider cost vs. rent increase potential
    • Focus on high-impact, low-cost improvements
  5. Marketing and Showing:
    • Take professional photos
    • Create compelling listing
    • Begin showing as soon as unit is presentable

6.4 Self-Management vs. Professional Management

Deciding whether to self-manage or hire a property manager is a critical decision that impacts your time, costs, and investment performance.

Self-Management Considerations

Advantages
  • Cost savings (typically 8-12% of monthly rent)
  • Direct control over tenant selection and property care
  • Immediate awareness of property issues
  • Personal relationship with tenants
  • Detailed knowledge of your investment
Disadvantages
  • Time-intensive commitment
  • 24/7 availability for emergencies
  • Emotional involvement in tenant issues
  • Learning curve for legal compliance
  • Geographic limitations (difficult for remote properties)
  • Challenging to scale with multiple properties
Best For
  • Investors with few properties
  • Local investors with available time
  • Those with property management experience
  • Hands-on personalities who enjoy direct involvement

Professional Management Considerations

Advantages
  • Time freedom for the investor
  • Professional expertise and systems
  • Established vendor relationships
  • Buffer between owner and tenant
  • Scalability for portfolio growth
  • Geographic freedom to invest anywhere
Disadvantages
  • Management fees (typically 8-12% of rent)
  • Leasing fees (often 50-100% of one month's rent)
  • Potential markup on maintenance
  • Less control over day-to-day decisions
  • Quality varies significantly between companies
  • Still requires owner oversight
Best For
  • Investors with multiple properties
  • Out-of-state or remote investors
  • Those with limited time or interest in management
  • Investors focused on scaling their portfolio

Selecting a Property Management Company

If you decide to hire professional management, a thorough selection process is essential:

Evaluation Criteria
  • Experience and Reputation:
    • Years in business
    • Number of units managed
    • Online reviews and ratings
    • References from current clients
  • Services Offered:
    • Tenant screening process
    • Rent collection methods
    • Maintenance handling
    • Eviction experience
    • Reporting and communication
  • Technology and Systems:
    • Online owner portal
    • Digital rent collection
    • Maintenance request tracking
    • Financial reporting capabilities
Fee Structure Analysis
  • Management Fee: Percentage of rent or flat fee
  • Leasing Fee: Cost to find and place tenants
  • Vacancy Fee: Charges during vacant periods
  • Setup Fee: Initial onboarding cost
  • Renewal Fee: Charge for lease renewals
  • Maintenance Markup: Percentage added to vendor bills
  • Inspection Fees: Charges for property inspections
  • Eviction Fee: Costs for handling evictions
  • Early Termination Fee: Cost to end management contract
Contract Considerations
  • Contract length and renewal terms
  • Termination clauses and notice periods
  • Performance guarantees
  • Maintenance spending authorization limits
  • Communication expectations

Hybrid Management Approaches

Some investors choose a middle path between full self-management and complete outsourcing:

Partial Management Services
  • Tenant Placement Only: Professional handles marketing, screening, and lease signing, then owner takes over
  • Maintenance-Only Service: Owner handles tenant relations, but maintenance calls go to a service
  • Rent Collection Service: Professional handles payments while owner manages property
  • On-Call Management: Self-manage with professional backup for vacations or emergencies
Technology-Assisted Self-Management
  • Property Management Software: Platforms like Buildium, AppFolio, or TenantCloud
  • Tenant Screening Services: TransUnion SmartMove or similar
  • Online Rent Collection: Specialized platforms or payment apps
  • Maintenance Coordination Apps: Services that dispatch vendors but owner maintains control
  • Virtual Assistants: Remote help for administrative tasks
